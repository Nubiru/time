# Key datasets on human time perception experiments to review

Key datasets from human time perception experiments focus on interval timing, duration estimation, and scalar properties, often available via open repositories for direct download and analysis.[^1][^2]

## Primary Open Repositories

The Timing Database is the standout resource, hosting raw data from 68+ datasets across eight tasks like interval reproduction, bisection, and temporal order judgments.[^1][^2]
Access it via its online GUI at the University of Groningen repository to filter, compile, and download standardized files for meta-analyses on accuracy, scalar variance, and precision malleability.[^1]
It supports submissions of new studies, enabling live growth for cross-study insights into human timing mechanisms.[^2]

## Notable Experiment Datasets

GitHub's prospective-retrospective dataset captures human responses in episodic memory and time perception tasks, tied to predictive processing models.[^3]
Download it directly for trial-by-trial data on duration biases from natural stimuli, useful for modeling subjective time distortions.[^3]
bioRxiv-linked video-watching experiments provide gaze-tracked duration estimates (1-64 seconds), testing perceptual classification against clock-like mechanisms.[^4]

## Specialized Studies

PLOS Computational Biology offers trial-by-trial brain activity data predicting subjective durations from visual cortex BOLD signals during natural videos.[^5]
Keele University's scalar timing resources include psychometric data series for modeling human interval estimation, emphasizing Weber's law compliance.[^6]
These complement broader collections by providing neural and behavioral traces for algorithmic replication.[^7]
